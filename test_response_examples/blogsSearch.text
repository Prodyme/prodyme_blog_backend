Keep in mind the existing decor, furniture, and the ambiance you want to create.\r\nColor trends keep coming and going, however some timeless examples are combinations are highlighting shades of beiges and browns along with majority of the wall and ceiling surfaces being white. This combination compliments well with wooden finishes of all shades and tones whether light woods or dark.\r\n\r\nAnother very ‘professional’ combination is the highlighter wall of light grey along with base tone around the room being white. \r\nThese two themes of browns and greys serve as a very versatile base and goes well with many colors of upholsteries, furniture finishes and accessories.\r\n\r\nThe choice of color theme of the room also depends on the age group that will predominantly be using the room. In India, the more mature the age group, the more ‘stable’ the colour them might need to be. This is especially true as it becomes difficult to coordinate colours with every bedsheet, decoratives, accessories and other stuff that this age group would have been gifted or collected themselves over the years. These are thoughts that one needs to put in while choosing the color them, and if this is done right, everything will fit in place.\r\nYounger people may choose from a plethora of colors and there really isn’t any ‘best’ one. Organisations such as Pantone or RAL and even paint companies such as Asian Paints come up with their ‘Color of the Years’ that might be a good place to start.\r\n\r\nLighting: Take into accountAccount for the natural and artificial lighting in the room; . Ddarker colors can make a room feel smaller, while lighter colors can make it appear more spacious. If the room has ample natural light sources then going for slightly darker tones would also work out. If there is a scope for planning artificial lights as well, then sky is the limit for how well the colors and textures can be made to pop. Please see our Guide to Great Lighting for more in-depth advice on planning the lighting in 3 layers- ambient, accents and pops.  \r\n\r\nFor choosing your paints however the following basics can help. Whichever wall is being directly light up by a natural source of light could be made to have a darker or ‘heavier’ color. The wall containing these windows, could be a lighter shade such as white or cream- this makes the windows appear bigger. If the room is bigger than 150sqft approx. then both the wall being directly lit by the windows and the wall behind the bed (or other focal furniture) can be heavily designed. Otherwise (if the room is small) limit to half height wall panels and light colored highlights behind the bed-wall.\r\n\r\n\r\nPersonal Preference: Different colors bring different emotionsased on the mood you want to evoke. Opt for calming colors like lavender, soft blues, and greens for a peaceful and relaxed vibe, and deeper hues like plum, dark teal, or blush pink for a romantic feel. A bright and cheerful atmosphere can be achieved with sunny yellows, light oranges, or minty greens. \r\n\r\nThere are various colour theories out in the market that can help us decide on such color schemes. Anyone trying to portray a high energy space can opt for the Complimentary Contrast options.\r\nIn this approach the colors chosen are opposite each other on the color wheel and create high contrast when used together. Complementary colors can make each other stand out and look balanced. They do this by stimulating different parts of the eyes equally, creating a natural illusion that adds energy and draws the eye. Such spaces and colors if used sparingly and thoughtfully create a really uniques experience. It is often better to limit these to smaller walls of larger rooms. Having large natural light sources is a must for long term functioning in the room. Guest bedrooms though are a good place to experiment such styles as lighting and even accessories can be well curated in such lesser used rooms. See our article on the psychology of colours. \r\n\r\nAnother such popular theory is the 60-30-10 principle is a classic decor rule that helps create a color palette for a space. It states that 60% of the room should be a dominant color, 30% should be the secondary color or texture and the last 10% should be an accent.\r\n\r\nAt the end of the day it is a mix of your life story that needs to be curated and you are the best artist for that.\r\n",

The ‘fresh paint smell’ that we are used to are actually these VOCs that we are smelling. Though the VOC emissions reduce over time, choosing paints that emit zero VOCs even just after painting can be chosen for families who are conscious of such exposure. Most paint shades are available in the non-VOC varieties. A lot of organizations and offices have mandated low VOC or zero VOC paints, especially those seeking green building ratings.
